# GTM

The exact target user is a founder-operator or head of engineering at a 10 to 75 person startup that has adopted several AI tools quickly, but does not yet have a dedicated procurement or finance ops person managing the sprawl. The most concrete buyer profile is a technical founder or engineering manager at a seed to Series A SaaS company with 5 to 25 developers and visible monthly spend across ChatGPT, Claude, Cursor, Copilot, Gemini, and direct APIs. They are close enough to budgets to care, but busy enough that they have not manually audited their stack.

Right before they want this tool, they are usually in one of three moments: renewal season, a surprise credit-card line item, or an internal conversation where someone asks why the company is paying for multiple overlapping AI subscriptions. The things they are likely to search or scroll right before finding value are very specific: “Cursor vs Copilot cost for team,” “ChatGPT Team vs Plus for startup,” “Claude Team pricing worth it,” “startup credits for OpenAI API,” or “too many AI subscriptions startup.” They are also likely to notice screenshots, cost breakdowns, or sharp founder threads about AI tool sprawl rather than generic productivity content.

The online channels that fit this user are not broad “startup Twitter” in the abstract. The better list is: r/SaaS, r/startups, r/Entrepreneur, Indie Hackers, smaller founder Slacks, AI engineering Discords, engineering-manager communities, and X lists that focus on startup ops, dev tools, and AI shipping. A good wedge is not “look at our cool AI tool,” but “we audited 20 startup AI stacks and found three repeat overspend patterns.” That gives a founder a reason to click because it sounds like a cost insight, not another chatbot wrapper.

For the first 100 users in 30 days with zero paid budget, I would do four things. First, post crisp public teardown screenshots of sample audit results, especially “before renewal” examples, on X and LinkedIn. Second, cold DM founders and engineering leads with a short message tied to a real signal like team size, active AI hiring, or a recent tooling post. Third, share the tool into communities where AI subscriptions and tooling spend are already being discussed. Fourth, publish a short teardown thread or blog post called something like “Five ways startup teams overpay for AI tools by accident” and use the audit as the free next step.

The unfair distribution channel for Credex is that this tool does not have to be a standalone product. Credex can pair it with startup credit workflows, vendor negotiation help, and real post-audit savings capture. That means the audit is not only content or top-of-funnel; it can connect directly to a service that helps teams actually realize the savings. That is more compelling than a generic calculator because it closes the loop from insight to action.

If week-1 traction is working, I would want to see something like 100 landing page visitors, 25 completed audits, 8 public shares, 6 captured leads, and 2 high-savings consultation requests. Those are small numbers, but they would show that the product is not just getting curiosity clicks; it is turning value moments into concrete pipeline.
